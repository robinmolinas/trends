## Definition
The transition of computational infrastructure from a globally optimized, hyperscaler-controlled commodity into a geographically and geopolitically constrained strategic resource — akin to energy, currency, or defense. "Compute Shock" is the sudden realization that AI and cloud services now require so much electricity and water that computation is no longer free to roam; instead, it anchors to locations with surplus power. "Sovereign AI" describes the nation-state and regional response: treating AI infrastructure as critical national infrastructure governed for control, resilience, and political alignment rather than traded as a cost-optimized commodity.

## Why It Matters
For nearly two decades, the digital economy ran on globally optimized cloud infrastructure controlled by a handful of hyperscalers. This model is fracturing. Single AI data centers now draw hundreds of megawatts, colliding with grid capacity. Frontier AI workloads double every 6–10 months. Access to affordable compute at scale is becoming unequal — larger countries and companies gain advantage; smaller actors face shrinking access. 

Two consequences compound: (1) global tech markets fracture into semiautonomous blocs with sovereign AI infrastructure, energy-tethered data centers, and geopolitical alignment requirements; (2) power accumulates in whoever controls the rails — the objective functions, default parameters, and interfaces — on which AI agents run.

## Tensions & Counterarguments
- Compute Shock logic benefits large incumbents and nation-states over agile newcomers — this concentrates power rather than distributing it
- Energy constraint from Compute Shock may actually accelerate renewable energy investment (Huawei's 50% wind/solar by 2035 prediction) — crisis → transition mechanism
- Huawei's 100,000× compute increase prediction implies the constraint is temporary → massive efficiency gains + energy grid AI integration solve it by 2035; FTSG is more pessimistic about the near-term timeline
