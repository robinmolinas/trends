## Definition
The structural transformation of the global beauty industry — historically organized around aesthetics and appearance — into a science-backed, health-led, life-centric category embedded within the broader health & wellness economy. Traditional beauty (cosmetics, fragrance, skincare for aesthetics) is losing category leadership to products and services that work "inside out": ingestibles, biotech skincare, wellness-integrated routines, and clinical/device aesthetics. This is described by Accenture as the "largest change in the past 20–30 years."

## Why It Matters
The global health & wellness market nearly doubles by 2030 ($1.7T → $3.1T), with beauty accounting for ~$0.83T of that figure. Beauty is no longer competing within a $427–580B cosmetics sector; it is competing for position within a $3.1T H&W market where nutrition, active lifestyle, and mental wellness are the primary growth categories. GLP-1 drugs (Ozempic/Wegovy) are directly reshaping the beauty and body care category as consumers' bodies and self-perception change. Gen AI is simultaneously restructuring beauty content, personalization, and product discovery. The combined result is an industry that must rebuild its portfolio, brand positioning, and channel strategy simultaneously.

## Tensions & Counterarguments
- "Science-backed beauty" risks becoming "sciencewashing" — the new marketing language replacing older sustainability and purpose claims without structural change
- AI-generated beauty content accelerates the "sameness" problem: faster content cycles paradoxically flatten differentiation at the mass market tier despite personalization promises
- BCG True Luxury data shows ultra-high-end beauty is intensely human-advisor-dependent — AI and science-led positioning may not translate to the luxury tier
