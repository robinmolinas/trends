## Definition

The accelerating shift away from a US-led unipolar global order toward a world with multiple competing power centers — primarily the US, China, and the EU, with the Global South, Middle East, and India gaining independent strategic weight. In business terms, multipolarity means: supply chains are no longer optimized purely for cost and speed; they must factor in geopolitical alignment, resilience, and national security requirements. The "rules" of the global marketplace (trade, investment, data, competition) are being rewritten simultaneously across multiple axes.

In 2026, this manifests as: a US tariff regime at its highest since 1934; a German €500B fiscal reset for defense and infrastructure; China controlling ~80% of rare earth mining while accounting for >25% of global industrial R&D; and AI becoming a defined axis of geopolitical competition with nation-states choosing AI supply chains as a matter of strategic interest.

## Why It Matters

The era of supply chains optimized purely for cost efficiency is over. BCG finds industrial-policy interventions motivated by national/economic security are up 6x since 2021. Companies must now maintain "cost of resilience" operating models — manufacturing and sourcing networks flexible enough to respond to geopolitical shocks without eroding margin. The AI dimension adds a new layer: relying solely on US or Chinese AI providers poses sovereignty risk in many markets.

For investors, this creates divergence: European defense spending is an investable theme; Global South growth (~50% of global economic growth by end of decade) represents a structural opportunity; and trade fragmentation creates sector-specific winners and losers based on supply chain exposure.

## Tensions & Counterarguments

- **Fragmentation vs. resilience**: BCG documents 6x increase in interventionist trade policies while Goldman Sachs CEO David Solomon argues for "resilient globalization" — trade is adapting, not collapsing. Both can be true simultaneously: trade volumes are resilient while the architecture is fragmenting.
- **AI sovereignty vs. AI efficiency**: Requiring national or regional AI supply chains adds cost and reduces capability access. The IBM finding that 93% of executives must factor AI sovereignty into strategy shows this is real, but the efficiency cost of fragmented AI ecosystems is not yet priced.
- **Climate divergence**: BCG notes energy security is overtaking decarbonization as a national policy driver — which fragments climate coordination. MSCI data shows institutional investors demanding comparable climate data, creating a private-capital vs. national-government tension.
