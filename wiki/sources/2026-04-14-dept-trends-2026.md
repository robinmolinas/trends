## Summary

DEPT®'s 2026 trends report (104 pages) frames the moment as a "growth invention" challenge: in an era where AI can replicate speed and efficiency, competitive advantage shifts to originality, adaptive strategy, and human meaning. The report is organized into four "invention" chapters — Inventing Brand, Inventing Culture, Inventing Commerce, and Inventing Systems.

The opening thesis is striking: brands are now building for two audiences simultaneously — humans and bots. AI assistants and search engines have become the primary *readers* of brand content; they don't see colors, mood boards, or manifesto copy. They read structure, clarity, and proof. Brands must treat language as infrastructure and evolve brand style guides into "codes of voice" with semantic HTML, schema markup, and consistent phrasing. A Cornell study found web pages with clear organization and strong content in 12+ of 16 key areas were 78% more likely to be cited by AI tools.

The Culture chapter identifies the "friction paradox" — consumers want convenience, but the right amount of friction fosters community, choice, and trust (the Labubu frenzy proves the thrill of the chase is itself valuable). This directly resonates with Accenture's Good Vibrations trend and Dentsu's same finding.

## Key Claims

- Brands now build for two audiences: humans and bots — language is infrastructure, not expression alone
- Pages with strong semantic structure are 78% more likely to be cited by AI tools (Cornell study)
- Proprietary first-party data is the new power play — third-party data erosion makes owned data the moat
- UX is evolving from frictionless to *trustworthy* — transparency, not just speed
- Creative alchemy: the human creative spark + AI execution velocity = the new competitive formula
- "Friction paradox" in commerce: zero-friction is not always optimal; meaningful friction creates community and desire
- GEO (generative engine optimization) is replacing SEO — AI discovery requires different content architecture
- AI is making brand insights generic at scale — the differentiation is the *human* judgment applied to AI outputs
