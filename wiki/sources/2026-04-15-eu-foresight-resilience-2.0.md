## Summary
The European Commission's 5th Strategic Foresight Report (manuscript completed September 2025) introduces "Resilience 2.0" — a leap from reactive crisis absorption to proactive, forward-looking resilience. Drawing on five recent major global crises (COVID-19, Ukraine war, Middle East situation, climate events, democratic threats) and seven long-term megatrends, the report argues the EU must transform its approach from "bounce back" to "bounce forward" as the geopolitical and technological environment becomes more volatile and unpredictable than ever.

The report is organized around: key long-term global developments, EU-specific challenges, and a path toward a resilient EU by 2040.

## Key Claims
- **Seven Megatrends shaping EU response**: Changing global order; changing security paradigm; accelerating climate change and environmental degradation; accelerating technological change; increasing demographic imbalances; widening inequalities; threats to democracy
- **Resilience 2.0 defined**: Not absorbing crises to maintain status quo — transforming and bouncing forward in a sustainable, fair and democratic manner. The shift from reactive to proactive/anticipatory governance.
- **Preparedness as a system**: Strategic foresight, situational awareness, and early warning must be strengthened as EU institutional capabilities — linked to the Niinistö preparedness strategy and European Preparedness Union Strategy
- **EU's unique scale as asset**: 450 million people, single market with 24 million companies and 15% of global goods trade — scale that no single member state achieves alone
- **Policy actions toward 2040 across key areas**: Technology sovereignty, demographic resilience, supply chain independence, energy security, defense industrial base, and democratic institution strengthening
- **Strategic foresight as competitive tool**: Countries that can anticipate and act pre-emptively on long-term trends gain decisive geopolitical advantage — foresight as geopolitical infrastructure

## Contradictions / Tensions
- "Proactive and forward-looking resilience" as a policy ideal conflicts with the EU's historical speed of regulatory and strategic response (slow-moving institutional machinery vs. fast-moving threats)
- The report advocates for strategic foresight as EU infrastructure — but its own assessment is that current EU foresight capabilities need strengthening, suggesting the gap is large
