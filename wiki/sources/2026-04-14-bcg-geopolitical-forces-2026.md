## Summary

BCG's December 2025 article (authored by six BCG partners including Nikolaus Lang and Aparna Bharadwaj) examines the geopolitical forces shaping the global business environment in 2026. The core thesis: the global shift toward multipolarity is accelerating and companies can no longer rely on the paradigms that have guided strategy for decades. "Longstanding rules and norms are changing, and a fluid patchwork of new rules and power dynamics appears to be emerging."

Six arenas of competition structure the analysis: (1) Trade and Foreign Direct Investment — WTO-based open marketplace being replaced by bilateral deals and tariff patchwork; (2) Tech and AI Race — US (59%) and China (26%) control the top-performing LLMs; "GenAI middle powers" (select European, Asian, Middle Eastern nations) are emerging; (3) Talent Scramble — US attracts 460K highly skilled people per year; India is the principal source of global STEM/AI talent; (4) Mission-Critical Sectors — industrial policy interventions up 6x since 2021; China controls ~80% of rare earth mining and ~85% of processing; (5) Climate Divergence — energy security eclipsing decarbonization as primary driver; China leads in renewables investment; (6) Expanding Conflicts — ~60 ongoing interstate and civil wars globally; highest since WWII.

## Key Claims

- More than 85% of global trade in merchandise does not involve the US — "friend-shoring" is expanding non-US trade networks
- US tariffs increased more than sixfold over the last 12 months
- US and China companies created 59% and 26% respectively of top-performing LLMs
- "GenAI middle powers" (Europe, Asia, Middle East) emerging as regional AI suppliers
- Industrial policy interventions motivated by national/economic security: up 6x since 2021
- China: manufacturing sector now double the size of the US; accounts for >25% of major industrial R&D
- China: controls ~80% of rare earth mining and ~85% of processing
- US: companies design >90% of advanced chips; leading developers of critical software
- Global talent: US attracted net 460,000 highly skilled people in 12 months to August 2025 (BCG Top Talent Tracker)
- UK attracted ~190,000 skilled immigrants in the same period
- India is the principal source of internationally mobile STEM and AI talent — 250,000+ highly skilled sent abroad
- ~60 ongoing interstate and civil wars globally — highest since WWII
- By end of decade, Global South will account for ~50% of global economic growth
- China: 887 gigawatts of solar power capacity installed at year-end 2024 — nearly double US + Europe combined
- US stablecoin market: Genius Act allows non-bank private entities to issue stablecoins

## Data Points

- LLM dominance: US 59%, China 26% of top-performing models (BCG analysis)
- Industrial policy interventions: 6x increase since 2021
- China solar capacity: 887 GW installed at year-end 2024
- US talent attraction: 460K highly skilled net inflow (BCG Top Talent Tracker, 12 months to Aug 2025)
- India talent export: 250K+ highly skilled people annually
- State-based conflicts: ~60 globally, highest since WWII

## Contradictions / Tensions

- BCG's framing of "GenAI middle powers" as viable regional AI suppliers is optimistic. Most evidence (GWI, Dentsu, others) suggests AI is currently a US-dominated infrastructure story. Whether European or Middle Eastern AI ecosystems can genuinely compete at model level is unproven.
- Climate divergence narrative (energy security > decarbonization in many countries) conflicts with MSCI's sustainability analysis which shows institutional investor demand for climate data is rising. Private capital markets and policy are moving in opposite directions.
