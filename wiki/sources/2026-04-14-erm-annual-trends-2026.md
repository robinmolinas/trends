## Summary

ERM's 61-page annual trends report organizes 2026 sustainability drivers around four macro trends, departing from their previous 10-heading format to reflect how sustainability has operationalized within corporate structures. ERM is one of the world's largest environmental and sustainability consultancies, giving this report a practitioner-grounded perspective distinct from investment bank outlooks.

The central argument: sustainability in 2026 is defined by competing pressures — political backlash vs. regulatory advancement, AI as both climate enabler and energy drain, tariffs putting sustainability budgets under pressure while simultaneously opening new commercial opportunities in climate adaptation. Rather than retreating, 83% of companies surveyed have increased sustainability spending.

The new dimension this year is EHS (Environment, Health, Safety) transformation as Trend Four — reflecting sustainability moving from strategy into operations, site management, and workforce safety.

## Key Claims

- 83% of companies globally increased sustainability spending in the past year; 87% of US companies maintained or boosted spending in 2025 despite political pushback
- 95% of companies view sustainability as a commercial opportunity; 99% expect it to deliver competitive advantage in the next 3 years
- 66% of sustainability executives at large US firms say tariffs will slow their sustainability progress
- $1.2 trillion in additional expenses expected by 9,000+ public companies in 2025 due to tariffs; $907B in lost profit
- In 2024, physical climate impacts cost the global economy at least $1.4 trillion (nearly 10x the 2000 total)
- Climate change may reduce global GDP by $23T by 2050 under current emissions trajectories
- 53% of companies reported operational impacts from climate-related events between June 2024 and June 2025; 75% believe physical climate risks will impact them in the next 5 years
- Revenue from climate adaptation products/services: ~$1.2T in 2024 (from ~2,100 companies), nearly double 2016; potentially $4T by 2050
- Global energy investment reached a record $3.3T in 2025 — $2.2T to low-carbon sources (2x fossil fuels)
- China controls ~60% of global critical mineral extraction and up to 90% of processing/refining capacity
- Critical mineral demand must triple by 2030 and quadruple by 2040 to meet net-zero goals (IEA)
- 77% of global institutional investors focusing sustainability engagements on issues with "clear financial relevance"
- 85% of chief investment officers willing to pay a premium for companies that clearly link sustainability to financial performance

## Contradictions / Tensions

- ESG regulatory fragmentation: simultaneous tightening in EU/Asia (CSRD, ISSB, new sustainability disclosure initiatives in Australia, Japan, Singapore) and rollback in US/UK
- The AI energy demand paradox: AI is simultaneously the most important tool for solving climate data complexity and a significant driver of the energy demand problem (data center power = significant % of grid)
- Companies are increasing sustainability investment while tariffs put those investments under financial pressure — the "investing under fire" dynamic
