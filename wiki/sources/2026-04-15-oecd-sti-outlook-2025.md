## Summary
OECD's biennial Science, Technology and Innovation (STI) Outlook (251 pages, approved September 2025) maps how STI systems are responding to a "shifting landscape" characterized by geopolitical fragmentation, AI transformation, the green transition, and post-pandemic institutional reconfiguration. The report analyzes policy trends across OECD member countries and provides statistical benchmarking of national innovation systems.

The 2025 edition particularly emphasizes how AI is simultaneously a transformative research tool and a source of systemic risk, and how geopolitical fragmentation is forcing countries to choose between openness and security in their science systems.

## Key Claims
- **AI as dual-use STI infrastructure**: AI is accelerating scientific discovery (drug design, materials science, climate modeling) while also concentrating power in a small number of actors and creating alignment/safety risks at the frontier
- **Geopolitical fragmentation of science**: Trade tensions, export controls, and security concerns are fragmenting the previously open global science system — "science decoupling" mirrors economic decoupling
- **Green transition as STI priority**: Climate technology and clean energy represent the largest sustained government-directed STI investment in decades; green patents growing across OECD
- **Post-pandemic STI recovery asymmetric**: Countries that maintained or increased public R&D investment during and after COVID are pulling ahead; those that cut back are falling behind in capability
- **Policy coherence gap**: STI policies increasingly entangled with industrial policy, security policy, and social policy — but governance structures remain siloed, reducing effectiveness
- **Open science expanding but contested**: Open science mandates growing globally, but national security concerns and commercial IP interests are creating new forms of selective closure

## Contradictions / Tensions
- OECD's open science advocacy conflicts with the report's own documentation of security-driven closures — this tension may not be resolvable through policy without fundamental geopolitical change
- The AI-accelerated science opportunity (drug discovery, climate modeling) requires massive compute access — which the Compute Shock convergence (FTSG) identifies as increasingly constrained and sovereignty-gated
