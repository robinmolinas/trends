## Summary

MSCI's Sustainability & Climate In Focus deck (presented at a London client event on December 3, 2025) provides institutional investors with MSCI's analysis of sustainability and climate trends for 2026. The presentation covers four main themes: investor demand for comparable and financially relevant ESG data; increasing exposure of private assets to catastrophic climate hazards; market forces keeping the energy transition moving; and central banks advancing on climate-risk oversight in a context of diverging national policies.

The private assets climate risk finding is significant: an increasing share of private assets is exposed to catastrophic climate hazards, with projected tropical cyclone losses escalating toward 2050 under a 3°C scenario. Infrastructure assets (both public and private) are showing increasing projected damage and business interruption costs. MSCI's analysis covers 2,308 infrastructure assets with ~$232B underlying holding valuation.

The state ownership theme is also highlighted: state ownership is on the rise globally, with MSCI flagging this as potentially negative for investors. Central bank climate risk oversight is advancing in some jurisdictions (UK, Europe, Australia) while diverging in others (US), creating a fragmented global banking regulatory environment on climate risk.

## Key Claims

- Institutional investors are demanding comparable, financially relevant climate data — ESG measurement standardization is accelerating
- Companies reporting detailed climate targets vary significantly by region: EMEA leads, Americas lags
- An increasing share of private assets is exposed to catastrophic climate hazards
- Projected tropical cyclone losses escalate toward 2050 under 3°C scenario (NGFS REMIND Current Policies)
- Infrastructure assets (private and public) showing growing projected damage and business interruption from climate events
- Market forces are keeping the energy transition moving even as policy diverges — technology commercial readiness is a performance driver
- Central banks in UK, Europe, Australia advancing on climate-risk oversight; US diverging
- State ownership is on the rise globally — flagged as potentially negative for investor returns
- MSCI database covers >12,000 issuers for climate data (as of October 2025)
- Employee turnover disclosure rates: 73–76% in EMEA; 49–58% in Americas for companies in MSCI ACWI Index

## Data Points

- MSCI climate database: >12,000 issuers as of October 2025
- Infrastructure assets analyzed: 2,308 assets (28% of peer group), underlying holding value ~$232B
- Employee turnover disclosure: 73–76% EMEA vs. 49–58% Americas (MSCI ACWI constituents)
- UK, Europe leading on climate risk management readiness for banks; US near bottom on prudential regulation stringency

## Contradictions / Tensions

- MSCI (serving institutional investors) shows strong demand for climate data and ESG standards, while BCG Geopolitics shows energy security eclipsing decarbonization as national priority in many markets. These reflect different principals: institutional capital markets (aligned with MSCI) vs. national governments (aligned with BCG's framing). The collision between capital market standards and national policy is one of the defining tensions of 2026.
- State ownership rising is flagged as negative for investors, but in the context of industrial policy interventions (BCG) and mission-critical sector designation, this is a structural trend unlikely to reverse.
